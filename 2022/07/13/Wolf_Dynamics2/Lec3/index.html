Introduction to turbulence modelling

  • The goal of turbulence modelling

    • develop equations that predict the time averaged primitive fields (velocity, pressure, temperature, concentration) without calculating the complete turbulent flow pattern as a function of time (from smalles to system scales)
    • find the closure equations to model the additional terms (usually a stress tensor)

  • Turbulence can orginate as

    • wall bounded
    • free shear
      • jets, mixing layers,heated walls, atmospheric flows

  • Turbulent motion has a direct effects on

    • the velocity profiles
      • in laminar case: the velocity gradient close to the walls is small (therefore the shear stresses are lower)
      • in the turbulent case: close to the walls with very large velocity gradients(large shear stresses); far from the wall where the velocity profile is nearly uniform

    • shear stresse

      • in a laminar flow or in the viscous sub-layer
      • in a turbulent flowNote:This approximation is valid for 2D boundary layers where $\tau^{R}=\overline{u’v’}$

    • mixing of transported quantities

Reynolds number and Rayleigh number

  • Reynolds number

    $U$: a charactersitic veloctiy, e.g. free-stream velocity
    $L$: a representative length sclae (or system scale), e.g., length, height, diameter, etc.

  • Rayleigh number

  • Grashof number
  • Prandtl number

Turbulent occurs at large Re

  • for external flow
    $\begin{aligned}
    Re_x & \ge 500000 &\qquad & \text{around slender/streamlined bodies (aerodynamic bodies)} \\ Re_d & \ge 20000 &\qquad & \text{around an obstacle (bluff bodies)}
    \end{aligned}$
    • for internal flow
      $Re_{d_h}\ge 2300$
    • for natural convection and buoyancy
      $\frac{Ra}{Pr} \ge 10^{9}$

Note: other factors may cause transition to turbulence at lower Reynolds number: free-stream turbulence, surface conditions, transpiration(blowing/suction),roughness…

Fluctuations of transported quantities

Note: impractial to account for all instantaneous fluctuations: remove,avoid,filter the small scales by usind models.

  • Important to understand

    • the time averaged flow pattern is a statistical property of the flow, not the actual flow pattern
    • the flow pattern changes from instant to instant

  • methods

    • Reynolds averaging (RANS/URANS)
      • all turbulence scales are modeled
      • 2D or 3D
      • steady or unsteady
    • Filtering (LES/DES)
      • resolves large eddies
      • models small eddies
      • intrinsically 3D and unsteady
  • conditions
    • averaging stationary turbulence
    • averaging nonstationary turbulence
    • statistically steady turbulence

Boundary layer

  • wall resolving simulation: simulate down to the viscous sublayer
  • wall modelling: model everthing below $y=30$

Scales of turbulence

  • Kolmogorov’s hypothesis
    • local isotropy: the small-scale turbulent motions are statistically isotropic (the same shape)
      1. similarity hypothesis: the small-scale motions determined by $\nu$ and $\epsilon$
      1. similarity hypothesis: the statistics of the motions between the large and small-scales determined by $\epsilon$, independt of $\nu$ 
![](/images/2022-07-13-16-02-20.png)
  • Figure:turbulence energy spectrum
    • the distribution of the turbulent kinetic energy across the various length scales
    • large wavenumber corresponds to small eddies with large frequencies (vers visa)
    • region A: energy containing eddies $L_0$
    • region B: inertial sub-range
    • regiong C: the eddies’ $Re_{T}$ is sufficiently small, molecular viscosity is effective in dissipating the kinetic energy

  • The motion at the smallest scales should depend only upon

    • The rate at which the larger eddies supply energy $\qquad \epsilon \left[ \frac{m^{2}}{s^{3}}\right]$: turbulent dissipation rate per unit mass
      $\qquad k \left[ \frac{m^{2}}{s^{2}}\right]$: turbulent kinetic energy per unit mass
    • The kinematic viscosity $\nu$

  • Kolmogorov scales: realations to determine the smallest scales in turbulence
    (new dimensional groups created with $\nu, \epsilon$)

    • Kolmogorov Reynodls number

      The energy cascade proceeds to smaller and smaller scales until the Reynolds number is small enough for dissipation due to viscosity to be effective.

      • turbulence Reynolds number $\boxed{Re_T\equiv \frac{k^{\frac{1}{2}}l_{0}}{\nu}}$
      • integral scales (length and time), $\boxed{l_0=\frac{k^{\frac{3}{2}}}{\epsilon}}, \tau_0=t=\frac{k}{\epsilon}$

      These relationships give you some idea about the meshes.

Integral length scale and grid length scale

  • the integral length scale $l_0$ can be estimated from a precursor RANS simulation
    • two-equation turbulence models
      • $k-\epsilon$
      • $k-\omega$
    • compute turbulent kinetic energy $k$, disispation rate $\epsilon$, and specific dissipation rate $\omega$
  • then compute integral length scale $l_0$

    Note:
    $l_0$ is telling you the region where occurs interaction of vorticies.
    It does not represent the dimension of the largest eddies or integral length scales.

  • then compute grid length scales
    • if $R_l$ is about $5-10$ in RANS, this is a good mesh -> you are resolving the integral length sclae $l_0$
    • $R_l$ criterion is used to study the core of the flow.
    • Close to the walls we use y+ as a criterion.

Turbulence near the wall

  • Definiton of $y^{+}$ and $u^{+}$

  • Law of the wall

    • The law of the wall basically describes the mean velocity distribution close to the wall, in the inner region of the boundary layer:
      • logarithmic layer
      • buffer layer
      • viscous sublayer

  • Logarithmic law or log-law

    • describe log-law layer for $30<y^{+}<300$
    • in reality, the upper limit can be much higher (10000) and depends on Re
    • in CFD, the upper limit is $600$

  • The viscous sublayer

    • valid in the viscous sublayer where laminar flow is very strong
      • the behavior of the mean velocity is linear in this region
    • $\tau_{\text{wall}}=\mu\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}$
    • in CFD, the upper limit $y^{+} <6$

  • The buffer layer

    • flow transition from laminar to turbulent
    • $5<y^{+}<30$
    • to avoid putting first cell center next to the wall in this region

  • Non-dimensional profiel vs physical velocity profile

Near-wall treatment and wall functions

  • Wall resolving

    • cluster at leat 5-10 layers at $y^{+}<5$
    • don’t be afraid to use this approach for 2D steady simulations

  • Wall functions

    • model everying below $y^{+}<30$
    • the first cell center located above $y^{+}>40-50$ and below $y\approx_0.2\delta_{99}$(boundary layer thickness)
    • 5-10 layers close to the wall in order to resolve the profile of the transported quantities
    • can be used in RANS, DES and LES
    • use this approach if you are more interested in the mixing in the outer region rather than de accurate prediction of forces on the walls
    • use this approach for unsteady cases

    • Launder and Spalding

      • therefore, introduce new paramters (independent of shear velocity)

        Note: if you have flow separation, wall modeling is not approprite.?1’50’. But there are models take into account the adverse gradient.

  • $y^{+}$ insensitive wall treatment

    • also called continuous wall functions / scalable wall functions

  • Removing small scales

RANS model

  • Starting equations: exact NES

    additional closure equations for the turbulence models

  • Exact Navier-Stokes equations (no models)

  • Incompressible RANS equations

  • Renolys stress

  • Boussinesq hypothesis: relate $\tau^{R}$ to the mean velocity gradient

    • strain-rate tensor $\overline{\bold{S}}^{R}=\frac{1}{2}\left[ \nabla \overline{\bold{u}}+\nabla \overline{\bold{u}}^{T}\right]$
    • identity matrix $\bold{I}$
    • turbulent eddy viscosity $\mu_t \qquad$ (not a fluid property, but needed by the turbulence model)
    • turbulent kinetic energy $k=\frac{1}{2}\overline{\bold{u’\cdot u}}$ or $k=\frac{1}{2}\left(\overline{u^{‘2}}+\overline{v^{‘2}}+\overline{w^{‘2}}\right)$

  • Solvable equations: RANS+Boussinesq

    • each turbulence model computes $\mu_t=f(k,\epsilon,\omega,l,t,v\cdots )$ in a different way

The $k-\omega$ family of Turbulence models

  • The closure equations of the turbulence problem using Reynolds average
    (scalar transport equation, y+ insensitive)

    • $\nu_t=\frac{k}{\omega}$
    • $\omega [\frac{1}{s}]$: specific rate of dissipation / dissipation frequency / vorticiy
    • $k=\frac{1}{2}\left(\overline{u^{‘2}}+\overline{v^{‘2}}+\overline{w^{‘2}}\right)$
    • closure coefficients $\alpha,\beta,\beta^{},\sigma,\sigma ^{}$

    • auxiliary relationships $\epsilon=\beta ^{*}\omega k \qquad l=\frac{k^{\frac{1}{2}}}{\omega}$

      Note:
      Each equation requires boundary conditions and Initial conditions
      This method can use wall function or can resolve the boundary layer

  • Using wall function for $k-\epsilon$

    • relate local wall shear stress (through $u_\tau$) to $U,k,\omega$
    • $u_\tau$ is solved iteratively by a small implicit system
    • $u_\tau \to k\to \omega$

  • Resolving the boundary layer

    • $\beta=0.075$
    • $y$: distance to the first cell center normal to the wall

  • The free-streeam initial conditions

    • set turbulence intensity level $I$
    • find the viscosity ration $\frac{\mu_t}{\mu}$
    • then get $\omega_\text{farfield}=\frac{\rho k}{\mu}\left( \frac{\mu_t}{\mu}\right)^{-1} \qquad k_{\text{farfield}}=\frac{3}{2}(UI)^{2}$

Comment:
k-omega does not perform well for rotationg flows
k-omega tends to be more instable

Workflow:

  1. do simulation with k-epsilon in a coarse mesh(But it has the problem with wall function)
  2. interpolate into a mesh for k-omega
  3. even move to LES

The $k-\epsilon$ turbulence model

  • $k-\epsilon$
    • $\nu_t=\frac{C_{\mu}k^{2}}{\epsilon}$
    • closure coefficients: $C_{\epsilon_1},C_{\epsilon_2},C_\mu,\sigma_k,\sigma_\epsilon$
    • auxiliary relationships: $\omega=\frac{\epsilon}{C_{\mu}k} \qquad l=\frac{C_\mu k^{\frac{3}{2}}}{\epsilon}$
    • the inaccuracies in the $k-\epsilon$ model stem from: $\nu_t$ computation and the turbulent dissipation rate equation

      Note:
      This module fix $C_\mu$ value. But it has been measured experimentally that this coefficient is not constant.
      To correct this problem: Low-Re $k- \epsilon$ with blending function.

  • $k-\epsilon$ model can only be used with wall functions
    • need to give physically realistic values of boundary conditions at the walls
    • standard wall function developed by Launder and Spalding
  • The free-stream initial conditions (see $k-\omega$ turbulence model)
  • The most notable limitation: it requires the use of wall functions

Low-Reynolds number $k-\epsilon$

  • low-Reynolds number refers to the Reynolds number measured normal to the wall (similar to the y+ normal to the wall), not the system Reynodls number
    • control the behavour close to the wall through $f_i,f_2,f_\mu,L_k,L_\epsilon$
      (correct or dampf the behavior of the turbulent viscosity as we approach to the walls)

General form of the transport equations of all turbulence models

- production term has same strucutre: 
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
            \text{Constant}& \times \tau_{ij}\frac{\partial \overline{u}_i}{\partial x_j}  \\ \text{Constant}& \times \bold{\tau}: \nabla \overline{\bold{u}}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    - constant x Reynolds stress tensor x mean velocity gradient
    - : scalar product of two second-rank tensors

$k-\omega$ SST turbulence model

  • can be seen as a hybrid method

    • away from the wall: standard $k-\epsilon$ model
    • close to the wall: $k-\omega$ model

  • The solvable equations of the $k-\omega$ SST

    • magnitude of the vorticity tensor: $\Omega=\sqrt{2W_{ij}W_{ij}}$
    • anti-symmetric part of the velocity gradient (vorticity tensor): $W_{ij}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\partial \overline{u}_i}{\partial x_j}-\frac{\partial \overline{u}_j}{\partial x_i}\right)$
    • a near-wall state $\phi_1$ and a far-wall state $\phi_2$: $\phi=F_1\phi_1+(1-F_1)\phi_2$
    • $F_1=\tanh(\text{arg}_1^{4}) \qquad 0\le F_1\le 1 $
    • $\text{arg}_1=\min \left[ \max \left(\frac{\sqrt{k}}{\beta^{*}\omega d}, \frac{500 \nu}{d^{2}\omega}\right), \frac{4\rho \sigma_{\omega 2}k}{CD_{k\omega}d^{2}} \right] \qquad \ge 0$

Wilcox 2006 $k-\epsilon$

  • The solvable equations

    • closure relation to compute th kinetmatic eddy viscosity:

    • cross-diffusion term:

      • to minimize the sensitivity of the model to free-stream boundary conditions in shear free regions
      • cross-diffusion coefficient $\sigma_d$
    • built-in stress limiter:
      • to improve the modeling capabilities when dealing with flow separation, incompressible free shear flows, and transonic/supersonic flows
      • activated when the magnitude of the strain rate is too large, which happens with separated flows, high Mach number flows, and in the presence of shock waves

The Spalart-Allmaras turbulence model

  • Based on the Boussinesq hypothesis

  • A closed equation for $\nu_t$ is artifically created that fits well a range of experimental and epmirical data (fully synthetic model)

  • It does not solve a transport equation for $\nu_t$, but a modified version of the turbulent eddy viscosity $\tilde{\nu}$
