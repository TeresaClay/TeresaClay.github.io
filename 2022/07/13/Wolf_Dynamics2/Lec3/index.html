Introduction to turbulence modelling

  • The goal of turbulence modelling

    • develop equations that predict the time averaged primitive fields (velocity, pressure, temperature, concentration) without calculating the complete turbulent flow pattern as a function of time (from smalles to system scales)

  • Turbulence can orginate as

    • wall bounded
    • free shear
      • jets, mixing layers,heated walls, atmospheric flows

  • Turbulent motion has a direct effects on

    • the velocity profiles
      • in laminar case: the velocity gradient close to the walls is small (therefore the shear stresses are lower)
      • in the turbulent case: close to the walls with very large velocity gradients(large shear stresses); far from the wall where the velocity profile is nearly uniform

    • shear stresse

      • in a laminar flow or in the viscous sub-layer
      • in a turbulent flowNote:This approximation is valid for 2D boundary layers where $\tau^{R}=\overline{u’v’}$

    • mixing of transported quantities

Reynolds number and Rayleigh number

  • Reynolds number

    $U$: a charactersitic veloctiy, e.g. free-stream velocity
    $L$: a representative length sclae (or system scale), e.g., length, height, diameter, etc.

  • Rayleigh number

  • Grashof number
  • Prandtl number

Turbulent occurs at large Re

  • for external flow
    $\begin{aligned}
    Re_x & \ge 500000 &\qquad & \text{around slender/streamlined bodies (aerodynamic bodies)} \\ Re_d & \ge 20000 &\qquad & \text{around an obstacle (bluff bodies)}
    \end{aligned}$
    • for internal flow
      $Re_{d_h}\ge 2300$
    • for natural convection and buoyancy
      $\frac{Ra}{Pr} \ge 10^{9}$

Note: other factors may cause transition to turbulence at lower Reynolds number: free-stream turbulence, surface conditions, transpiration(blowing/suction),roughness…

Fluctuations of transported quantities

Note: impractial to account for all instantaneous fluctuations: remove,avoid,filter the small scales by usind models.

  • Important to understand

    • the time averaged flow pattern is a statistical property of the flow, not the actual flow pattern
    • the flow pattern changes from instant to instant

  • methods

    • Reynolds averaging
    • Filtering
  • conditions
    • averaging stationary turbulence
    • averaging nonstationary turbulence
    • statistically steady turbulence

Boundary layer

  • wall resolving simulation: simulate down to the viscous sublayer
  • wall modelling: model everthing below $y=30$

Scales of turbulence

  • Kolmogorov’s hypothesis
    • local isotropy: the small-scale turbulent motions are statistically isotropic (the same shape)
      1. similarity hypothesis: the small-scale motions determined by $\nu$ and $\epsilon$
      1. similarity hypothesis: the statistics of the motions between the large and small-scales determined by $\epsilon$, independt of $\nu$ 
![](/images/2022-07-13-16-02-20.png)
  • Figure:turbulence energy spectrum
    • the distribution of the turbulent kinetic energy across the various length scales
    • large wavenumber corresponds to small eddies with large frequencies (vers visa)
    • region A: energy containing eddies $L_0$
    • region B: inertial sub-range
    • regiong C: the eddies’ $Re_{T}$ is sufficiently small, molecular viscosity is effective in dissipating the kinetic energy

  • The motion at the smallest scales should depend only upon

    • The rate at which the larger eddies supply energy $\qquad \epsilon \left[ \frac{m^{2}}{s^{3}}\right]$: turbulent dissipation rate per unit mass
      $\qquad k \left[ \frac{m^{2}}{s^{2}}\right]$: turbulent kinetic energy per unit mass
    • The kinematic viscosity $\nu$

  • Kolmogorov scales: realations to determine the smallest scales in turbulence
    (new dimensional groups created with $\nu, \epsilon$)

    • Kolmogorov Reynodls number

      The energy cascade proceeds to smaller and smaller scales until the Reynolds number is small enough for dissipation due to viscosity to be effective.

      • turbulence Reynolds number $\boxed{Re_T\equiv \frac{k^{\frac{1}{2}}l_{0}}{\nu}}$
      • integral scales (length and time), $\boxed{l_0=\frac{k^{\frac{3}{2}}}{\epsilon}}, \tau_0=t=\frac{k}{\epsilon}$

      These relationships give you some idea about the meshes.

Energy spectrum and mesh resolution

  • Culmulative plot of the turbulent kinetic energie


  • resolve at least 80% of the turbulent kinetic energy $k(l)$ (spectrum)
  • some parameters
    • $\kappa=\frac{2\pi}{l}=\frac{2\pi f}{U}$:wavenumber
    • $l:$ the smallest vortex that can be resolved with the mesn (characteristic eddy size)
    • $l_0:$ largest eddies or integral length scales
    • $\Delta=\frac{l}{2}:$ cell size;grid cut-off dimension; the smallest eddies you can resolve with two cells

  • to resolve an eddy with a length scale $l$

Integral length scale and grid length scale

  • the integral length scale $l_0$ can be estimated from a precursor RANS simulation
    • two-equation turbulence models
      • $k-\epsilon$
      • $k-\omega$
    • compute turbulent kinetic energy $k$, disispation rate $\epsilon$, and specific dissipation rate $\omega$
  • then compute integral length scale $l_0$

    Note:
    $l_0$ is telling you the region where occurs interaction of vorticies.It does not represent the dimension of the largest eddies or integral length scales.

  • then compute grid length scales
    • if $R_l$ is about $5-10$, this is a good mesh -> you are resolving the integral length sclae $l_0$

      Note:
      The $R_l$ criterion is used to study the core of the flow.
      Close to the walls we use y+ as a criterion.

Turbulence near the wall

  • Definiton of $y^{+}$ and $u^{+}$

  • Law of the wall

    • The law of the wall basically describes the mean velocity distribution close to the wall, in the inner region of the boundary layer:
      • logarithmic layer
      • buffer layer
      • viscous sublayer

  • Logarithmic law or log-law

    • describe log-law layer for $30<y^{+}<300$
    • in reality, the upper limit can be much higher (10000) and depends on Re
    • in CFD, the upper limit is $600$

  • The viscous sublayer

    • valid in the viscous sublayer where laminar flow is very strong
      • the behavior of the mean velocity is linear in this region
    • $\tau_{\text{wall}}=\mu\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}$
    • in CFD, the upper limit $y^{+} <6$

  • The buffer layer

    • flow transition from laminar to turbulent
    • $5<y^{+}<30$
    • to avoid putting first cell center next to the wall in this region
