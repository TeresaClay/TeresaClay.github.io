The turbulent world around us

  • turbulence (turbulent flows) in fluid fluid dynamics
    • characterize a certain type of flow
    • the counterpart of a streamline motion, smooth flow, or a laminar flow

characteristics of turbulent flows

  • irregularity (randomness)
    • described statistically
  • diffusivity
    • case rapid mixing and increased rates of momentum, heat and mass transfer

A flow that looks rand but does not exhibit the spreading of velocity fluctuations through the surrounding fluid is not turbulent.

  • dissipative

    • kinetic energy gets converted into heat due to viscous shear stresses

  • high Reynolds numbers

    • a compex interaction between the viscous forces and convection
  • rotational (non-zero vorticity)
  • continuum
    • even the smallest eddies are significantly larger thant the molecular scales
  • a feature of fluid flow
    • not a property of the flow

small summary

  • unsteady, aperiodic motion in which all three velocity components fluctuate in space and time
  • every transported quantity shows similar fluctuations (pressure, temperature, species, concentration…)
  • contains a wide range of eddy sizes (scales)
    • Large eddies
      • derives their energy from the mean flow
      • are unstable and break into smaller eddies
    • Small eddies
      • convert kinetic energy into thermal energy via viscous dissipation
      • their behavior is more universal in nature

Introduction to turbulence modelling

  • The goal of turbulence modelling

    • develop equations that predict the time averaged primitive fields (velocity, pressure, temperature, concentration) without calculating the complete turbulent flow pattern as a function of time (from smalles to system scales)

  • Turbulence can orginate as

    • wall bounded
    • free shear
      • jets, mixing layers,heated walls, atmospheric flows

  • Turbulent motion has a direct effects on

    • the velocity profiles
      • in laminar case: the velocity gradient close to the walls is small (therefore the shear stresses are lower)
      • in the turbulent case: close to the walls with very large velocity gradients(large shear stresses); far from the wall where the velocity profile is nearly uniform

    • shear stresse

      • in a laminar flow or in the viscous sub-layer
      • in a turbulent flowNote:This approximation is valid for 2D boundary layers where $\tau^{R}=\overline{u’v’}$

    • mixing of transported quantities

Reynolds number and Rayleigh number

  • Reynolds number

    $U$: a charactersitic veloctiy, e.g. free-stream velocity
    $L$: a representative length sclae (or system scale), e.g., length, height, diameter, etc.

  • Rayleigh number

  • Grashof number
  • Prandtl number

Turbulent occurs at large Re

  • for external flow
    $\begin{aligned}
    Re_x & \ge 5000 &\qquad & \text{around slender/streamlined bodies (aerodynamic bodies)} \\ Re_d & \ge 2300 &\qquad & \text{around an obstacle (bluff bodies)}
    \end{aligned}$
    • for internal flow
      $Re_{d_h}\ge 2300$
    • for natural convection and buoyancy
      $\frac{Ra}{Pr} \ge 10^{9}$

Note: other factors may cause transition to turbulence at lower Reynolds number: free-stream turbulence, surface conditions, transpiration(blowing/suction),roughness…

Boundary layer

  • viscous boundary layer in the velocity distribution

  • momentum boundary layer

  • thermal boundary layer
    • thickness is different from the other two boundary layers
    • thickness (of the thermal sublayer) is fluid dependent
      • hight Prandtl number fluid (water): much less
      • Prandtl number =1: equal to the momentum boundary layer

Scales of turbulence

  • Kolmogorov’s hypothesis
    • local isotropy: the small-scale turbulent motions are statistically isotropic
      1. similarity hypothesis: the small-scale motions determined by $\nu$ and $\epsilon$
      1. similarity hypothesis: the statistics of the motions between the large and small-scales determined by $\epsilon$, independt of $\nu$
