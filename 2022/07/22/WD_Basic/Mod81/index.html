    Introduction to turbulence modelling

    • Turbulence can orginate as

      • wall bounded turbulence
      • shear free turbulence
        • jets, mixing layers,heated walls, atmospheric flows

    • Turbulent motion has a direct effects on

      • the velocity profiles

      • shear stresse

        • in a laminar flow or in the viscous sub-layer
        • in a turbulent flowNote:This approximation is valid for 2D boundary layers where $\tau^{R}=\overline{u’v’}$

      • mixing of transported quantities

    Reynolds number and Rayleigh number

    • Reynolds number

      $U$: a charactersitic veloctiy, e.g. free-stream velocity
      $L$: a representative length sclae (or system scale), e.g., length, height, diameter, etc.

    • Rayleigh number

    • Grashof number
    • Prandtl number

    Removing fluctuations of transported quantities

    • Reynolds averaging (RANS/URANS)
      • all turbulence scales are modeled
      • 2D or 3D
      • steady or unsteady
    • Filtering (LES/DES)
      • resolves large eddies
      • models small eddies
      • intrinsically 3D and unsteady
    • conditions
      • averaging stationary turbulence
      • averaging nonstationary turbulence
      • statistically steady turbulence

    Scales of turbulence

    • The motion at the smallest scales should depend only upon

      • The rate at which the larger eddies supply and $\nu$ $\qquad \epsilon \left[ \frac{m^{2}}{s^{3}}\right]$: turbulent dissipation rate (per unit mass)
        $\qquad k \left[ \frac{m^{2}}{s^{2}}\right]$: turbulent kinetic energy (per unit mass)

    • Kolmogorov scales: to determine the smallest scales in turbulence (at which the energy is disispated)

    • Kolmogorov Reynolds number and Turbulent disssipation rate
    • Integral scales: characteritsic length and time scales of turbulence producing eddies $l_0$

    • combine previous two scales

      These relationships give you some idea about the meshes

    • Turbulence Reynolds number (Re of the integral length)

    • Turbulent kinetic energie (TKE)

    • Interial range: between integral scale and Kolmogorov scale

      • motion of the eddies are determined by inertial effects (viscous effects are negligible)

    - under equilibrium conditions, turbulence production = turbulence dissipation

    Integral length scale and grid length scale

    • the integral length scale $l_0$ can be estimated from a precursor RANS simulation (to get $k, \epsilon,\omega$)

    • then compute integral length scale $l_0$

      Note:
      $l_0$ is telling you the region where occurs interaction of vorticies.
      It does not represent the dimension of the largest eddies or integral length scales.

    • then compute

    • $R_L>5-10$

      • RANS/URANS/VLES: at least 5-6 cells to resolve $l_0$
      • SRS/LES: at least 10 cells to resolve $l_0$ (resolve 80% of the turbulence spectrum)

    Turbulence near the wall

    • Definiton of $y^{+}$ and $u^{+}$

    • Law of the wall

      1. Logarithmic law or log-law

      2. The buffer layer

      3. The viscous sublayer

    • Spalding’s law: universal velocity profile fits the laminar, buffer and logarithmic regions

    Near-wall treatment and wall functions

    • Wall resolving - low-RE(LRN)

      • cluster even 15-30 layers for $\color{red}{y^{+}<6-10}$
      • don’t be afraid to use this approach for 2D steady simulations

    • Wall functions - high-RE(HRN)

      • model everying below $\color{red}{y^{+}<30}$
      • the first cell center located above $\color{blue}{y^{+}>40-50}$ and below $\color{blue}{y\approx 0.2\delta_{99}}$(boundary layer thickness)
      • 5-10 layers close to the wall in order to resolve the profile of the transported quantities
      • use this approach for unsteady cases
      • can be used in RANS, DES and LES
      • Launder and Spalding
        • in a separation point, $\tau_{w}=0 \to U_{\tau}=0 \to $ a singularity in $u^{+}=\frac{U}{U^{\tau}}$
        • therefore, introduce new paramters (independent of shear velocity)

    • $y^{+}$ insensitive wall treatment

      • also called continuous wall functions / scalable wall functions
      • valid in the whole boundary layer, $\color{red}{1<y^{+}<300-600}$
      • 8-10 layers to resolve the profile of transported quantities

    Incompressible RANS equations

    • Starting equations: exact NES (DNS)

      additional closure equations for the turbulence models

    • DNS (exact NS equations)

    • Incompressible RANS equations (governing equaiton)

    • Renolys stress tensor

      • RSM (Reynolds stress model): derive eqn for each term and resolve them (avoid Boussinesq hypothesis)

    • Boussinesq hypothesis: relate $\tau^{R}$ to the mean velocity gradient

      • strain-rate tensor
      • turbulent kinetic energy
      • each turbulence model will compute turbulent eddy viscosity $\mu_t$ in a different way
        (not a fluid property, but needed by the turbulence model)

    Solvable equations: RANS+Boussinesq

    • The problem now reduces to computing the turbulent eddy viscosity $\mu_t=f(k,\epsilon,\omega,l,t,v\cdots )$ (closure problem)

    The $k-\omega$ family of turbulence models

    • The closure equations of the turbulence problem (scalar transport equation)

    • or

    • with auxiliary relationships

    • with

      • $k=\frac{1}{2}\left(\overline{u^{‘2}}+\overline{v^{‘2}}+\overline{w^{‘2}}\right)$
      • $\omega [\frac{1}{s}]$: specific turbulence dissipation rate / dissipation frequency / vorticiy
      • $\epsilon [\frac{m^{2}}{s^{3}}]$: turbulence dissipation rate
      • $l$: integral length scales
      • closure coefficients $\alpha,\beta,\beta^{},\sigma,\sigma ^{}$

      Note:
      Each equation requires boundary conditions and Initial conditions
      This method can use wall function or can resolve the boundary layer

    This family of models is $y^{+}$ insensitive

    • Standard wall functions for $k-\epsilon$
      • relate local wall shear stress (through $u_\tau$) to $U,k,\omega$
      • $u_\tau$ is solved iteratively by a small implicit system
      • $u_\tau \to k\to \omega$
    • Resolving the boundary layer
      • $\beta=0.075$
      • $y$: distance to the first cell center normal to the wall

    The $k-\epsilon$ turbulence model

    • $k-\epsilon$

    • with auxiliary relationships

      • closure coefficients: $C_{\epsilon_1},C_{\epsilon_2},C_\mu,\sigma_k,\sigma_\epsilon$

      • the inaccuracies in the $k-\epsilon$ model stem from: $\nu_t$ computation and the turbulent dissipation rate equation

    Note:
    This module fix $C_\mu$ value. But it has been measured experimentally that this coefficient is not constant.
    To correct this problem: Low-Re $k- \epsilon$ with blending function.

    • $k-\epsilon$ model can only be used with wall functions
      • need to give physically realistic values of boundary conditions at the walls
      • standard wall function developed by Launder and Spalding
    • The free-stream initial conditions (see $k-\omega$ turbulence model)
    • The most notable limitation: it requires the use of wall functions

    Generalization of $k-\epsilon$ turbulence model

    • control the behavour close to the wall through $f_1,f_2,f_\mu,L_k,L_\epsilon$
      (correct or dampf the behavior of the turbulent viscosity as we approach to the walls)
      • low-Reynolds number refers to the Reynolds number measured normal to the wall (similar to the y+ normal to the wall), not the system Reynodls number

    $k-\omega$ SST turbulence model

    • can be seen as a hybrid method

      • away from the wall: standard $k-\epsilon$ model
      • close to the wall: $k-\omega$ model

    • no need to use damping functions near the wall

    • The solvable equations of the $k-\omega$ SST

      • magnitude of the vorticity tensor: $\Omega=\sqrt{2W_{ij}W_{ij}}$
      • anti-symmetric part of the velocity gradient (vorticity tensor): $W_{ij}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\partial \overline{u}_i}{\partial x_j}-\frac{\partial \overline{u}_j}{\partial x_i}\right)$
      • a near-wall state $\phi_1$ and a far-wall state $\phi_2$: $\phi=F_1\phi_1+(1-F_1)\phi_2$
      • $0\le F_1\le 1 $

    • The wall boundary conditions

    Wilcox 2006 $k-\epsilon$

    • It has the same predicting capabilities as $k-\omega$ SST mode, but wihtout complex blending functions
    • The solvable equations

    • with closure relation to compute th kinetmatic eddy viscosity:

    • auxiliary relationships

    • cross-diffusion term:

      • to minimize the sensitivity of the model to free-stream boundary conditions in shear free regions
      • cross-diffusion coefficient $\sigma_d$
    • built-in stress limiter:
      • to improve the modeling capabilities when dealing with flow separation, incompressible free shear flows, and transonic/supersonic flows
      • activated when the magnitude of the strain rate is too large, which happens with separated flows, high Mach number flows, and in the presence of shock waves
      • closure coefficients

    LES (large eddy simulation)

    • LES decomposition (filtering decomposition)
    • $\text{GS}$:
      • grid scales
      • filtered quantity or resolved field
      • already contains fluctuations
      • the mesh or filter is able to solve the fluctuations
    • $\text{SGS}$:
      • sub-grid scales, small sclaes,
      • residual quantity,or unresolved fields
      • what is modelled (can not be resolved with the filter)
    Wokrflow - start with RANS compute the intergral length,y+ - then move the LES ### FNS quations (filtered incompressible Navier-Stokes equation) $$ \begin{aligned} \nabla \cdot (\overline{\boldsymbol{u}})&=0 \\ \frac{\partial \overline{\boldsymbol{u}}}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot (\overline{\boldsymbol{u}}\,\overline{\boldsymbol{u}})&=\frac{-\nabla \overline{p}}{\rho}+\nu\nabla ^{2}\overline{\boldsymbol{u}}+\color{red}{\underbrace{\nabla \cdot \tau^{SGS}}_{\color{black}{-(\overline{\boldsymbol{uu}}-\overline{\boldsymbol{u}}\,\overline{\boldsymbol{u}})}}} \end{aligned} $$ - **sub-grid scale stress tensor** $$ \begin{aligned} \tau^{\text{SGS}}&=-(\overline{\boldsymbol{uu}}-\overline{\boldsymbol{u}}\,\overline{\boldsymbol{u}})=\overline{\boldsymbol{u}}\,\overline{\boldsymbol{u}}-\overline{\boldsymbol{uu}} \end{aligned} $$ > Note: The integral length scales in RANS/URANS are pretty much grid independent. You are not going to resolve more coherent structures using finer and finer meshes. ### LES sub-grid scale models - model the whole sub-grid scale stress tensor $\tau^{\text{SGS}}$ through Boussinesq hypothesis $$ \begin{aligned} \tau^{\text{SGS}}&=-(\overline{\boldsymbol{uu}}-\overline{\boldsymbol{u}}\,\overline{\boldsymbol{u}})=\overline{\boldsymbol{u}}\,\overline{\boldsymbol{u}}-\overline{\boldsymbol{uu}}=-2\nu_\text{SGS}\overline{\boldsymbol{S}} \\ \tau^{\text{SGS}}&=(\overline{\overline{\boldsymbol{u}} \, \overline{\boldsymbol{u}} }-\overline{\boldsymbol{u}}\,\overline{\boldsymbol{u}})+(\overline{\overline{\boldsymbol{u}}\boldsymbol{u^{'}}})+\overline{\boldsymbol{u^{'}u^{'}}}=-2\nu_\text{SGS}\overline{\boldsymbol{S}} \end{aligned} $$ #### Smagorinsky model (not recommended) $$ \begin{aligned} \overline{\boldsymbol{S}}&=\frac{1}{2}\left( \nabla \overline{\boldsymbol{u}}+\nabla \overline{\boldsymbol{u}}^{T} \right) \\ \nu_{\text{SGS}}&=\left( C_s \Delta \right)^{2}\left\vert \overline{\boldsymbol{S}} \right\vert =l^{2}\left\vert \overline{\boldsymbol{S}} \right\vert \end{aligned} $$ - $\Delta $: filter width (proportional to the cell spacing) $\qquad (\Delta _x \Delta _y \Delta _z)^{\frac{1}{3}}=\text{cell volume}^{\frac{1}{3}}$ - $C_s$: Smagorinsky constant -> needs to be calibrated (**drawback**) - $\left\vert \overline{\boldsymbol{S}} \right\vert=\left(2\overline{\boldsymbol{S}}:\overline{\boldsymbol{S}}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}$ > Note: We can see that in LES everthing depens on grids $\Delta$

    • The sub-grid scal eddy viscosity relation resembles a mixing length formulation

    • Problem: close to the wall, $\nu_\text{SGS}$ can be incorrectly overpredicted (similar to LR $k-\epsilon$) - fruther improved

      Note:Smagorinsky model is good for shear free flows, but not good for wall-bounded flow.
